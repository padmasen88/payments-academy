ACH — Overview & History

The Automated Clearing House (ACH) network is the backbone of everyday US money movement — responsible for direct deposit payroll, Social Security benefits, bill pay, business-to-business payments, and the invisible settlement layer underneath consumer apps like Zelle and Venmo. Governed by Nacha (formerly NACHA — the National Automated Clearing House Association), ACH processed 31.5 billion transactions totaling $80.1 trillion in 2023 alone.

1968
Origin: The concept of an electronic check clearing system emerges as banks seek to reduce the cost and volume of paper checks.
1972
First ACH Association: The California ACH Association is formed. GIRO Association follows. Regional ACH associations begin operating across the US.
1974
NACHA Founded: The National Automated Clearing House Association is established to create uniform rules and governance across regional associations.
1996
Internet era: WEB entry type introduced, enabling internet-initiated ACH debits for online bill payment.
2016
Same-Day ACH launches: Phase 1 enables same-day ACH credits. Revolutionary for payroll, insurance, and urgent B2B payments.
2022
Limit increases: Same-Day ACH limit raised to $1,000,000 per transaction, dramatically expanding its B2B use case.
2026
Present: ACH coexists with RTP and FedNow, serving as the workhorse for scheduled and bulk payments where speed is secondary to cost.

ACH vs. Wire: A Common Misconception

ACH is often conflated with wire transfers. The critical difference: ACH is a batch, deferred-settlement, reversible system. Wires are individual, real-time, irrevocable. ACH is low-cost (~$0.02–0.25 per transaction); wires are expensive ($15–50+).

1.2

Participants & Roles

Every ACH transaction involves five core participants. Understanding their roles and liability is essential to understanding the ACH risk framework.

🏭
Originator
Company or individual who initiates the ACH transaction.

Examples: Your employer (for payroll), Netflix (for subscription billing), a landlord (for rent). The originator has an agreement with the ODFI.

🏦
ODFI
Originating Depository Financial Institution

The bank or credit union that receives payment instructions from the originator and submits them into the ACH network. The ODFI bears significant liability for unauthorized or erroneous entries.

⚙️
ACH Operator
Central clearing facility for ACH transactions.

Two operators: FedACH (Federal Reserve Banks) handles ~60% of volume. EPN (Electronic Payments Network, operated by The Clearing House) handles ~40%. They interoperate.

🏛
RDFI
Receiving Depository Financial Institution

The receiver's bank. Receives ACH entries from the operator and posts them to the receiver's account. Responsible for returning entries within required timeframes.

👤
Receiver
The account holder at the RDFI.

Can be a consumer or a business. Has authorized the originator to debit or credit their account, typically via a signed authorization agreement.

🔗
Third-Party Sender (TPS)
Intermediary between originator and ODFI.

Payment processors (e.g., Stripe, Dwolla) that aggregate ACH transactions on behalf of many originators. Subject to strict Nacha registration and compliance requirements since 2022.

⚠️

ODFI Liability: A Critical Concept

The ODFI is ultimately liable to the ACH network for all entries it submits. If an originator generates fraudulent or unauthorized entries, the ODFI must make the affected parties whole. This is why ODFIs perform extensive due diligence and ongoing monitoring of their originators and third-party senders.

The Dual Operators & Interoperability

ACH entries can be processed by either operator (FedACH or EPN). Banks are members of one or both. Interoperability rules ensure that an ODFI on EPN can still pay a receiver at a bank that only uses FedACH. The operators exchange files through established exchange agreements.

ACH Debit Flow (e.g., Bill Pay)

1

Consumer Authorizes

Consumer provides bank routing + account number to the biller (e.g., utility company). The authorization can be written, electronic (WEB), or verbal (TEL). The originator must retain proof of authorization.

2

Biller Creates ACH Debit Entry

On the due date, the biller (originator) creates an ACH debit entry targeting the consumer's account. This is a CCD (business) or PPD (consumer) SEC code entry.

3

ODFI Submits Batch

The biller's bank (ODFI) aggregates entries into a batch file and submits to the ACH operator at a designated cut-off time. Files have a rigid NACHA format (94-character fixed-width records).

4

Operator Sorts & Distributes

The ACH operator validates the file format, sorts entries by receiving bank (RDFI), and distributes them. FedACH settles through the Federal Reserve's National Settlement Service. EPN settles through its own settlement process.

5

RDFI Posts or Returns

The receiver's bank posts the debit to the consumer's account. If the account has insufficient funds (NSF), the account is closed, or the transaction is unauthorized, the RDFI returns it with a return code (R01, R02, etc.) within 2 business days.

6

Settlement

For standard ACH, funds settle the next banking day after origination. For Same-Day ACH, settlement occurs on the same banking day within the defined processing windows.

WEB Debit Validation Rule (2021)

Since March 2021, Nacha requires originators of WEB debit entries to use a "commercially reasonable" method to validate that a routing and account number are for a valid, open account. Methods include micro-deposit verification, instant account verification (IAV) via Plaid/MX, or real-time account validation services. This rule significantly impacted fintech ACH origination practices.

1.5

Processing Windows & Timing

ACH does not process transactions individually or continuously. It operates on defined processing windows. Understanding these windows is critical for product design and customer expectations.

Standard ACH (Next-Day Settlement)

The traditional ACH processing model. Entries submitted by the ODFI's cut-off time are sorted by the operator overnight and delivered to the RDFI the following banking day for settlement. Weekends and Federal Reserve holidays cause delays.

📅

Why Next-Day ACH Matters for Payroll

For standard payroll, employers typically originate ACH files 2 banking days before payday (e.g., originate Wednesday for Friday payday). Some forward-dated entries allow submission further in advance. Banks that "early-fund" payroll are taking on credit risk for the advance.

Same-Day ACH (SDA) — Launched 2016

Same-Day ACH was phased in by Nacha and allows for same-banking-day settlement. All Nacha-member banks are required to receive same-day ACH entries (credits from 2016; debits from 2017). Three processing windows allow multiple intraday submissions:

Window ODFI Submission Deadline (ET) RDFI Settlement Funds Availability
Morning Window 10:30 AM ET 1:00 PM ET No later than 1:30 PM ET
Afternoon Window 2:45 PM ET 5:00 PM ET No later than 5:00 PM ET
Evening Window 4:45 PM ET 6:00 PM ET End of business day (or next morning)

Same-Day ACH: Limits & Surcharges

  • Per-transaction limit: $1,000,000 (raised from $25K → $100K → $1M through successive rule changes)
  • SDA surcharge: ODFIs pay RDFIs a $0.052 per-entry surcharge for same-day entries (mandated by Nacha rules)
  • Not all entries eligible: IAT (international) and some government entries are excluded from Same-Day ACH
  • Irrevocability caveat: Same-day ACH credits CAN still be returned (up to 2 banking days for consumer entries, 2 banking days for business)

1.6

Return Codes

When an ACH entry cannot be posted — because the account is closed, insufficient funds exist, or the transaction was unauthorized — the RDFI sends back a return entry with a standardized return code. Understanding return codes is critical for payment operations teams managing ACH programs. Returns must typically be transmitted within 2 banking days of settlement (60-day returns apply only to consumer unauthorized entries under specific conditions).

Most Common Return Codes

R01
Insufficient Funds
Available balance insufficient to cover the debit. Most common return. May indicate NSF or uncollected funds.
R02
Account Closed
Account has been closed by the depository institution or the customer. Do not re-present; update records.
R03
No Account / Unable to Locate
Account number does not match any account at RDFI. Could indicate a data entry error or fraudulent account info.
R04
Invalid Account Number
Account number structure is not valid. The RDFI cannot post; check account formatting.
R05
Unauthorized Debit to Consumer
A CCD or CTX debit was applied to a consumer account without authorization. High-risk code for ODFIs.
R07
Authorization Revoked
Consumer revoked the authorization. Originator must cease debiting immediately. Continued debits after R07 = Nacha violation.
R08
Payment Stopped
Receiver placed a stop payment on this entry. Similar to a check stop payment. Must honor for at least 6 months.
R09
Uncollected Funds
Collected balance sufficient but uncollected funds present. The RDFI may re-present; funds may clear later.
R10
Customer Advises Unauthorized
Consumer advises the debit was unauthorized. Nacha rule: RDFI has up to 60 calendar days from settlement to return for consumer unauthorized entries.
R11
Check Truncation Entry Return
Used when returning ARC, BOC, or POP entries. Original check item was ineligible.
R16
Account Frozen
Account access restricted due to legal order, regulatory action, or internal bank hold. Common in BSA/AML freeze scenarios.
R20
Non-Transaction Account
Account type does not allow ACH (e.g., savings account with Reg D restrictions, or brokerage account).
R29
Corporate Not Authorized
Corporate receiver has not authorized this debit (equivalent of R10 for businesses). Must return within 2 banking days.
🚨

Return Rate Monitoring — Nacha Compliance

Nacha imposes strict return rate thresholds. ODFIs must monitor originator return rates:
• Overall ACH debit returns: Must not exceed 15% of debit entries
• Unauthorized returns (R05, R07, R10, R29): Must not exceed 0.5% of debit entries
• Administrative returns (R02, R03, R04): Must not exceed 3% of debit entries
Exceeding these thresholds triggers ODFI review and potential suspension of origination rights.

1.7

Risk, Fraud & Compliance

🔓 Key Fraud Vectors

  • Unauthorized ACH debits: Fraudster uses stolen bank credentials to pull funds
  • Account takeover (ATO): Attacker gains access to online banking and initiates unauthorized ACH transfers
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC): Attacker impersonates executive or vendor and tricks AP department into sending ACH
  • Synthetic identity fraud: Fraudulent account opened with fabricated identity; credit obtained and account drained via ACH
  • Kiting: Exploiting float between banks by cycling ACH transfers to manufacture fake balances

📜 Key Regulations

  • Nacha Operating Rules: The comprehensive rulebook governing all ACH participants
  • Reg E (EFTA): Consumer protections for electronic fund transfers including ACH
  • Reg CC: Funds availability rules (how quickly banks must make ACH credits available)
  • BSA/AML: Bank Secrecy Act requirements for transaction monitoring and SAR filing
  • OFAC: Sanctions screening required on all ACH entries (mandatory for IAT)
  • UDAAP: CFPB unfair/deceptive/abusive acts practices — applies to ACH origination practices

Nacha Third-Party Sender (TPS) Requirements

Since 2022, Nacha requires Third-Party Senders (payment processors) to formally register with the ACH network. TPS must:
(1) Register annually with Nacha with legal entity info, list of ODFI relationships, and industries served;
(2) Comply with all Nacha rules including return rate monitoring;
(3) Be subject to Nacha audits and enforcement actions. This significantly impacted fintech ACH platforms like Dwolla, Stripe ACH, and others.

Credit Risk: ODFI Exposure on ACH Credits

When an ODFI allows an originator to send ACH credits (like payroll) before funding settles, the ODFI extends intraday credit. If the originator's settlement account comes up short, the ODFI absorbs the loss. ODFIs manage this through: prefunding requirements, exposure limits, collateral, and creditworthiness analysis of the originator.

RTP — Overview & History

The Real-Time Payments (RTP) network is the US's first new payment rail in four decades, launched by The Clearing House (TCH) in November 2017. Unlike ACH — which batches and defers — RTP is a 24/7/365, always-on, individual transaction system that settles each payment in seconds and provides immediate confirmation. RTP is a credit-push only network: funds can only be pushed to a recipient; debits (pulling from someone's account) are not supported.

Who Operates RTP?

The Clearing House (TCH) — a private banking association — owns and operates the RTP network. TCH is owned by the largest US commercial banks (Bank of America, Citibank, JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, US Bank, etc.). This private ownership was a key driver for the Federal Reserve creating FedNow as a public alternative.

2015

Nov 2017

2020
RTP reaches 50% of US DDA (demand deposit accounts) coverage. Limit raised to $100,000.
2022
Limit raised to $1,000,000. RTP now viable for large commercial payments and B2B.
2023
RTP reaches ~300 financial institution participants covering 65%+ of US DDAs. Competes directly with newly launched FedNow.

Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS)

RTP uses a real-time gross settlement model — each transaction settles individually (gross), not netted against others. Settlement occurs in the TCH multilateral net settlement account at the Federal Reserve. Each RTP participant maintains a pre-funded settlement account at the Fed (called a "joint account"). When a payment is sent, the sending bank's balance in the joint account decreases and the receiving bank's increases — instantly.

ISO 20022 Messaging Standard

RTP messages use the ISO 20022 financial messaging standard — a rich, XML-based format that carries far more payment data than legacy formats (like ACH's flat-file NACHA format or SWIFT MT messages). ISO 20022 enables structured remittance data (invoice numbers, line items, references) to travel with the payment — a major advantage for B2B reconciliation.

✅

Request for Payment (RFP)

RTP includes a powerful feature: Request for Payment (RFP). A payee can send an electronic payment request to a payer, who then authorizes a credit push. This enables use cases like: B2B electronic invoicing, utility bill pay, and consumer billing — all without the payer sharing their account details. The payer retains control; there are no ACH debits involved.

2.4

Adoption, Limits & Use Cases

Adoption Challenges

Despite launching in 2017, RTP adoption among US banks has been slower than expected. Key barriers:

  • Prefunding requirement: Banks must maintain funded balances in the TCH joint settlement account 24/7. For smaller banks, this is a liquidity management challenge.
  • Core banking integration: Banks need real-time integration with their core banking systems (often legacy batch-processing systems) to post payments instantly, day and night.
  • Cost: RTP transaction fees are higher than ACH (~$0.045 per transaction vs. ~$0.02 for ACH).
  • Receive-only: Many banks have enabled receiving RTP payments but not sending — a common asymmetric adoption pattern.
  • Private operator concern: Community banks and credit unions were hesitant to fund a TCH-owned network, preferring to wait for the Fed's alternative (FedNow).

FedNow — Overview

FedNow is the Federal Reserve's instant payment service, launched in July 2023. It is the first new Federal Reserve payment rail since FedACH and represents a significant strategic decision: the US government decided the private-sector RTP network should have a public-sector counterpart to ensure universal access, particularly for community banks and credit unions that were reluctant to rely solely on TCH.

🏛

Why the Fed Built FedNow

The Federal Reserve argued that a single private-sector instant payment system (RTP) could create systemic concentration risk and that smaller financial institutions deserved a public alternative they trusted. Community banks and credit unions had advocated strongly for Fed involvement. FedNow is designed to coexist with RTP, not replace it.

FedNow Architecture

FedNow is technically a real-time gross settlement (RTGS) system, similar to RTP. Each participating bank holds a master account at the Federal Reserve. When a FedNow payment is initiated, the Fed debits the sending bank's master account and credits the receiving bank's master account in real time. This gives FedNow a structural advantage: Federal Reserve master accounts are the ultimate settlement asset — there is no credit risk between the two banks in a FedNow transaction.

Liquidity Management Tool

FedNow offers a Liquidity Management Tool (LMT) that allows banks to shift funds between their FedNow master account balances and other Fed accounts (like the overnight fed funds accounts) to manage intraday liquidity. This is critical for community banks managing 24/7 liquidity without the infrastructure of large banks.

The Interoperability Gap

A significant limitation: FedNow and RTP do not interoperate. A bank only on FedNow cannot receive an RTP payment, and vice versa. For full coverage, banks (or payment providers) must participate in both networks. This fragmentation is a key industry debate — the Fed and TCH have discussed interoperability but no timeline exists as of 2025.

Wire Transfers — Fedwire & CHIPS

Wire transfers are the high-value, same-day, irrevocable payment rail. Unlike ACH (batch, reversible) or RTP (real-time, limited to $1M), wires are used when speed and certainty matter most and dollar amounts are large — think real estate closings, M&A transaction funding, interbank settlements, and large corporate treasury operations. Wires move trillions of dollars daily through two primary domestic networks: Fedwire Funds and CHIPS.

Fedwire Funds Service

Operated by the Federal Reserve Banks, Fedwire is a real-time gross settlement (RTGS) system. It settles each wire individually and immediately by debiting and crediting the Federal Reserve master accounts of the participating banks. Fedwire operates on business days from 9:00 PM ET (prior day) to 7:00 PM ET (approximately). Nearly every bank in the US has access to Fedwire, either directly or through a correspondent bank.

  • Participants: ~5,800 depository institutions, directly or via correspondents
  • Settlement: Real-time gross in Federal Reserve master accounts
  • No limit: Fedwire has no per-transaction dollar limit
  • Format: Fedwire uses its own proprietary message format (Fed IMAD tracking numbers)
  • Fees: Banks typically charge consumers $15-50 per wire. Federal Reserve charges member banks $0.57–$0.82 per transaction

CHIPS (Clearing House Interbank Payments System)

Also operated by The Clearing House, CHIPS is the primary large-value payment system for USD-denominated international wire transfers. CHIPS uses a multilateral netting algorithm — rather than settling each transaction gross, it continuously nets offsetting payments between banks, dramatically reducing the liquidity required to settle the day's transactions.

  • Participants: ~45 major US and international banks (the largest global banks)
  • Netting efficiency: CHIPS nets approximately 95% of transaction value — meaning $1.8T in daily transactions requires only ~$90B in actual fund movement
  • Final settlement: At end of day, CHIPS net positions settle over Fedwire
  • Primary use: International USD payments, foreign exchange settlements, eurodollar transactions
  • Operating hours: Opens 12:01 AM ET; closing routine begins ~5:00 PM ET
💡

Fedwire vs. CHIPS: When to Use Which

Fedwire: Used when the sender or receiver is not a CHIPS participant, for domestic high-value transactions, or when same-day real-time gross settlement is required. Typically: domestic real estate, legal settlements, tax payments, payroll wire funding.
CHIPS: Used for international USD transactions, foreign exchange settlements, and large interbank transactions between CHIPS participants. More liquidity-efficient due to netting.

SWIFT — The International Messaging Layer

SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) is a messaging network, not a payment system. SWIFT does not move money — it transmits payment instructions between banks around the world. The actual settlement of international wires occurs over national RTGS systems (like Fedwire in the US, TARGET2 in Europe, CHAPS in the UK) using correspondent banking relationships.

How SWIFT Works

1

Message Initiation

The sending bank generates a SWIFT message (historically MT103 for customer payments, now migrating to ISO 20022 MX messages). The message contains the beneficiary's IBAN, BIC code, amount, currency, and payment details.

2

Correspondent Routing

If the sending and receiving banks don't have a direct relationship, the payment routes through one or more correspondent banks — banks that have accounts with both parties (nostro/vostro accounts). Each hop introduces fees and potential delays.

3

Settlement

Each correspondent bank settles with the next through their bilateral nostro/vostro account balances or through their national RTGS system. The final leg delivers funds to the beneficiary bank.

4

Credit to Beneficiary

The receiving bank credits the beneficiary account. International wires typically take 1-5 business days, depending on the number of correspondent hops, cut-off times, and compliance screening (OFAC, AML).

SWIFT GPI (Global Payments Innovation)

Launched 2017, SWIFT GPI dramatically improved international wire speed and transparency. GPI enables same-day cross-border settlement for many corridors, tracking via a unique end-to-end transaction reference (UETR), and full fee transparency. Over 90% of SWIFT transactions now use GPI.

SWIFT Sanctions & Geopolitics

SWIFT is a neutral cooperative but has become geopolitically significant. Russia was partially disconnected from SWIFT in 2022 following the Ukraine invasion — a powerful economic sanction that illustrated how central SWIFT is to global financial connectivity. Russia responded by expanding the SPFS (System for Transfer of Financial Messages) domestically.

Key SWIFT Concepts

TermDefinition
BIC / SWIFT Code Bank Identifier Code — 8 or 11 character code identifying bank, country, location, and branch. Used to route SWIFT messages.
IBAN International Bank Account Number — standardized account identifier used in Europe and many other regions. Not used in the US domestically.

Wire Fraud, Risk & Compliance

Wire Fraud: The Highest-Stakes Payment Crime

Wire fraud carries the most severe financial consequences of any payment fraud type — because wires are irrevocable and dollar amounts are large. Once a wire settles, recovery requires the cooperation of the receiving bank and, in many cases, law enforcement.

🎭 Business Email Compromise (BEC)

The FBI's IC3 reports BEC as the #1 cybercrime by dollar losses — over $2.9B in 2023. Attacker impersonates a CEO, CFO, or vendor via spoofed or hacked email. Tricks AP or finance staff into wiring funds to a fraudulent account. The FBI's REACT (Recovery Asset Team) can claw back funds if notified within hours, but success rate drops sharply after 72 hours.

🏠 Real Estate Wire Fraud

Fraudster intercepts email between homebuyer and title company, substitutes fraudulent wire instructions for the closing payment. Homebuyer sends down payment or closing funds to the fraudster's account. Devastating and often unrecoverable. Industry response: voice confirmation protocols, encrypted wire instruction delivery.

Compliance Requirements

  • UCC Article 4A: The primary US legal framework for wire transfers (wholesale funds transfers). Defines rights and obligations of banks and customers in wire transactions.
  • OFAC screening: Every wire must be screened against OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list. Matches must be blocked and reported.
  • BSA/AML monitoring: Large wire transactions ($10,000+) may trigger CTR (Currency Transaction Report) requirements. Suspicious patterns trigger SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) filings.
  • FinCEN Travel Rule: For wire transfers over $3,000, the bank must pass along originator information (name, account, address) to the next bank in the chain.
  • Dual control: Best practice for large wires — two authorized employees must approve before initiation.
  • Callback verification: Best practice — always call back the wire recipient at a known number (not from the request itself) to verify instructions before sending.

Choosing the Right Rail

📅
Use ACH When...
Timing is flexible (overnight is OK), volume is high, cost sensitivity matters most, or you need debit (pull) capability — payroll, recurring billing, B2B bulk payments.
⚡
Use RTP When...
Instant confirmation matters, 24/7 availability is needed, amount is under $1M, and you want rich ISO 20022 remittance data — earned wages, claims, gig payouts, B2B invoice pay.
🏛
Use FedNow When...
Your bank or receiver's bank is a FedNow participant (not on RTP), amounts are under $500K, and you prefer Federal Reserve settlement certainty.
🔒
Use Wires When...
Dollar amount exceeds RTP/FedNow limits, transaction is international, same-day certainty is legally required (real estate closings), or counterparty requires wire.
