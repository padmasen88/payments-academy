The Four-Party Model

The dominant structure of the US card payments industry is the four-party model — a system where four distinct entities participate in every transaction. Understanding who they are, what they do, and how money flows between them is the foundation of card payments literacy.

Three-Party Model (Closed Loop): Amex & Discover

American Express and Discover have historically operated three-party (closed-loop) models where they act as both issuer AND acquirer. This gives them total control of the network and higher per-transaction economics, but limits acceptance (merchants pay higher MDR). Amex has increasingly opened to third-party issuers (e.g., Delta SkyMiles issued by Amex, but also co-brands issued by other banks like Hilton Honors Aspire by Amex directly). Discover was acquired by Capital One in 2024 — a transformative industry event.

1.2

Authorization Flow

Authorization is the real-time process of verifying a card transaction before funds are committed. It happens in under 2 seconds end-to-end, traversing four parties across the globe. Understanding this flow is critical for anyone building card-based products.

1

Card Presented at Terminal

Consumer taps (NFC/contactless), inserts (EMV chip), or swipes (magstripe) their card at the merchant's point-of-sale terminal. The terminal reads the card data — PAN (Primary Account Number), expiry date, and a cryptographic verification value. For EMV chip transactions, the chip generates a unique cryptogram per transaction (preventing counterfeit cloning).

2

Acquirer Submits Authorization Request

The terminal sends the transaction data to the payment processor / acquirer via an ISO 8583 message. The acquirer adds merchant data (MCC, merchant ID, terminal ID) and routes the authorization request to the appropriate card network based on the card's BIN (Bank Identification Number — first 6–8 digits of the PAN).

3

Network Routes to Issuer

Visa or Mastercard's switching infrastructure identifies the issuing bank from the BIN table and routes the authorization request to the issuer's authorization system in real time. The network also applies its own fraud screening (Visa Advanced Authorization, Mastercard Safety Net) and can decline transactions before they reach the issuer.

4

Issuer Makes the Decision

The issuer's authorization system evaluates the transaction in milliseconds against:
• Available balance / credit limit
• Fraud score (proprietary ML models scoring 100+ signals: location, velocity, MCC, device, behavioral patterns)
• Velocity rules (e.g., no more than 5 transactions in 10 minutes)
• Account standing (delinquency, account restrictions)
• Card status (not reported lost/stolen)
Response codes: 00 = Approved, 05 = Do Not Honor, 51 = Insufficient Funds, 54 = Expired Card, 57 = Transaction Not Permitted, 62 = Restricted Card.

5

Response Returns to Terminal

The authorization response (approved/declined + response code) routes back through the network to the acquirer to the terminal. Elapsed time: typically 1–2 seconds. An approval places an authorization hold on the funds — reducing available balance, but not yet settling. No money has moved yet.

6

Authorization Hold Dynamics

Authorization holds can be tricky for consumers. Examples:
• Gas stations: Pre-auth of $1 or $100 (to verify card); adjusted to actual amount after fill-up
• Hotels: Pre-auth for estimated stay + incidentals; may be $200–$500 over actual room rate
• Car rentals: Large pre-auth holds entire security deposit
Holds that are never settled (because the transaction doesn't complete) fall off after the issuer's hold expiry period (typically 3–7 days for most merchants; up to 30 days for travel).

💡

Stand-In Processing (STIP)

When a card issuer's authorization system is down (planned maintenance or outage), the card network steps in with Stand-In Processing. Visa or Mastercard uses stored issuer parameters to approve or decline transactions on the issuer's behalf. This prevents merchant disruption but creates risk: the network approves based on last-known parameters, which may not reflect current account status.

1.3

Clearing & Settlement

Authorization and settlement are completely separate processes. Authorization happens in real time; clearing and settlement happen in batch, typically T+1 or T+2. This distinction is critical for treasury management, reconciliation, and understanding the timing of merchant funding.

Clearing

At end of day, the merchant's POS system sends a batch close — submitting all the day's authorized transactions to the acquirer for clearing. Each transaction is matched against its authorization record. This is the process of officially recording the transaction details through the network.

Settlement

After clearing, funds flow as follows:

1

Issuer to Network

The issuing bank pays the card network the gross transaction amount.

2

Network Nets and Pays Acquirer

The network nets all transactions, deducts interchange (which flows back to the issuer), deducts its own assessment fees, and pays the acquirer the net amount.

3

Acquirer Pays Merchant

The acquirer deposits funds to the merchant's bank account, net of the Merchant Discount Rate (MDR). Most merchants receive funds T+1 or T+2 after batch close.

💡

The Interchange Flow Is Counter-Intuitive

Many people assume interchange flows from issuer to acquirer. The direction is actually acquirer → network → issuer. The merchant pays the MDR to the acquirer; the acquirer remits interchange to the network; the network passes it to the issuer. This is why the issuer — who takes the credit risk — earns interchange. The merchant bears the cost; the cardholder gets the rewards funded by that cost.

Gross vs. Net Settlement

Merchants receive net of fees — the MDR is already deducted before funds hit the merchant's account. For a $100 transaction with a 2.5% MDR, the merchant receives $97.50. This contrasts with ACH and wire where the full amount is sent and fees are charged separately.

Durbin Amendment (Debit Interchange Cap)

Enacted as part of Dodd-Frank in 2010, implemented by Federal Reserve Regulation II in 2011, the Durbin Amendment was a watershed moment in payments regulation. It caps debit interchange for large issuers and introduced mandatory network routing choice for merchants.

  • Interchange cap: For issuers with assets >$10 billion: maximum debit interchange = $0.21 + 0.05% of transaction amount + $0.01 fraud prevention adjustment
  • Dual-network routing: Every debit card must carry two unaffiliated network brands (e.g., Visa + Star). Merchants choose which to route through — and most choose the cheaper PIN network.
  • Small bank exemption: Issuers under $10B in assets are nominally exempt; they can earn market-rate interchange (~$0.50–$0.80 per transaction). In practice, networks maintain higher rates for small issuers to preserve the appearance of the exemption.
  • Revenue impact: Durbin cost large bank debit issuers an estimated $8–10 billion per year in lost interchange. Banks responded by eliminating free checking accounts, adding fees, and increasing minimum balance requirements.
  • Durbin 2.0 (2023 Fed proposal): The Federal Reserve proposed extending routing requirements to card-not-present (e-commerce) transactions — a major ongoing battle between networks, issuers, and merchants.

BIN / IIN Structure

The Bank Identification Number (BIN) — also called the Issuer Identification Number (IIN) — is the first 6 to 8 digits of a card's Primary Account Number (PAN). It is the routing key of the card payments world, encoding a remarkable amount of information used in every transaction.

What a BIN Encodes

🌐 Network Identification

  • 4x xxxx — Visa
  • 51–55 or 2221–2720 — Mastercard
  • 34, 37 — American Express
  • 6011, 644–649, 65 — Discover
  • 35 — JCB (Japanese Credit Bureau)
  • 62 — UnionPay

🏦 Issuer & Card Details

  • Specific issuing bank (Chase, Citi, BofA)
  • Card product type (debit/credit/prepaid)
  • Reward tier (standard, gold, platinum, signature)
  • Country of issuance
  • Currency of billing
  • Whether card is commercial or consumer

16-Digit Card Structure

4532 15 ← BIN (6 digits) 78 9012 345 ← Account Number 6 ← Luhn Check Digit

Luhn Algorithm

The final digit of every card number is a check digit calculated using the Luhn algorithm (also called Mod 10). It's a simple checksum that allows any system to validate whether a card number is structurally valid without contacting the network. While it prevents accidental data entry errors, it does not prevent fraud — fraudsters know the algorithm and generate valid-format card numbers easily.

Extended BINs (8-digit)

The industry is migrating from 6-digit to 8-digit BINs (mandated by ISO 2021). As the number of card products grew, the 6-digit BIN space became insufficient to uniquely identify all issuers and products. The migration to 8-digit BINs (with 10-digit account number) maintains a 16-digit PAN but provides vastly more routing capacity. This migration has significant operational implications for acquirers, processors, and any system that parses PANs.

Regulation E (EFTA) — Deep Dive

The Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA), implemented by the Federal Reserve's Regulation E, is the primary consumer protection framework for electronic fund transfers — including debit card transactions, ACH, and prepaid cards. Every payments practitioner must know its key provisions.

Unauthorized Transaction Liability Limits

ScenarioConsumer LiabilityBank Obligation
Report within 2 business days of learning of lossMaximum $50Bank covers the rest
Report 3–60 days after statement with errorMaximum $500Bank covers amounts over $500
Report after 60 days from statementUnlimitedBank may not be required to cover
Timely report; no negligence$0 (full bank coverage)Bank absorbs full loss

Bank Investigation Requirements

  • 10 business days: Bank must complete investigation within 10 business days of receiving dispute
  • Provisional credit: If bank needs more than 10 days, it must provide provisional credit to the consumer within 10 days and can extend investigation to 45 days
  • 45 days (POS/foreign): For POS or foreign transactions, the investigation window extends to 45 days if provisional credit is given (90 days for new accounts)
  • Notification: Bank must notify consumer of the outcome of the investigation

Regulation E — Overdraft Opt-In Rule

A critical 2010 amendment: Under Reg E, consumers must affirmatively opt in to overdraft coverage for one-time debit card transactions and ATM withdrawals. Without opt-in, the bank must decline debit transactions that would overdraw the account (no fee charged; consumer is simply declined). For ACH and checks, banks can still charge overdraft fees without opt-in. This rule significantly reduced bank overdraft fee revenue from debit card transactions.

⚠️

Reg E Does NOT Cover Business Accounts

Regulation E protections apply to consumer accounts only. Business debit card disputes are governed by commercial bank agreements (typically UCC Article 4) and have far less protective rules. This is why business account holders have much shorter dispute windows and must implement stronger internal controls.

2.3

The Durbin Amendment — Deep Dive

The Durbin Amendment (Dodd-Frank Act Section 1075, implemented as Federal Reserve Regulation II) is arguably the most consequential piece of US payments regulation in decades. Championed by Senator Dick Durbin of Illinois, it was a direct response to merchant lobbying about high debit interchange costs — and fundamentally restructured the debit card industry.

📉 The Interchange Cap

For covered issuers (assets >$10B): Maximum debit interchange = $0.21 + 0.05% of transaction + $0.01 fraud adjustment. This replaced pre-Durbin rates of $0.44 average per transaction. The cap reduced issuer revenue by ~50% on debit interchange.

🔀 Network Routing (Section 920)

Every debit card must have two unaffiliated network brands. Merchants have the right to route to the network of their choice. The practical result: merchants route to cheaper PIN networks (Star, NYCE, Pulse) over Visa/MC, saving significant interchange costs.

Small Bank Exemption — The Reality

The law nominally exempts issuers under $10 billion in assets from the cap. But in practice, networks created dual interchange schedules — one capped rate for large issuers, one higher "exempt" rate for small issuers. Merchants and acquirers route debit cards to whichever network provides the rate they prefer. Small issuers have found that routing to the regulated interchange schedule still happens frequently, eroding their theoretical exemption benefit.

Durbin's Ripple Effects

  • End of free checking: Large banks (BofA, Chase, Wells) eliminated free checking or added minimum balance requirements to recoup lost interchange revenue
  • Debit rewards eliminated: Virtually all debit card rewards programs at large banks were discontinued — debit interchange can no longer fund them at Durbin-capped rates
  • PIN terminal investment: Merchants heavily invested in PIN-capable terminals to route to cheaper PIN networks
  • Fintech/small bank advantage: Neobanks (Chime, Current, Dave) partner with banks under $10B (e.g., Bancorp Bank, MetaBank) to offer debit cards with higher interchange — funding their reward programs
  • The Durbin 2.0 debate: The Fed's 2023 proposed rule would extend routing rights to CNP (e-commerce) transactions — currently, Visa/MC dominate online debit routing. Merchants strongly support; networks and issuers strongly oppose.

2.4

Debit Fraud & Security

🦹 Fraud Typologies

  • Card skimming: Physical overlay device on ATM or gas pump reads magstripe data. Card is cloned for fraudulent use.
  • Card shimming: Thin device inserted into chip card slot intercepts EMV chip data. More sophisticated than skimming.
  • ATM jackpotting: Malware installed on ATM causes it to dispense cash at command.
  • CNP fraud: Stolen card data used for online purchases (card not physically needed).
  • Account takeover: Attacker gains online banking credentials, changes PIN, adds new card to digital wallet.

🔒 Security Technologies

  • EMV chip (2015 US liability shift): Generates unique cryptogram per transaction. Makes card-present counterfeiting extremely difficult.
  • Contactless (NFC): Tokenized — actual PAN never transmitted. Extremely secure for card-present.
  • Network tokenization: PAN replaced with a network token for digital wallet storage. Merchant-specific tokens; useless if stolen.
  • Velocity monitoring: Issuers flag unusual geographic or spending patterns in real time.
  • Device fingerprinting: For CNP transactions, device data helps identify suspicious sessions.
💡

The 2015 EMV Liability Shift

Before October 2015, card issuers bore the cost of counterfeit card fraud. The US EMV liability shift changed this: if a merchant does not have a chip-capable terminal and a counterfeit EMV chip card is used, the merchant (not the issuer) bears the fraud loss. This drove rapid deployment of chip terminals across US merchants — from ~50% in 2015 to 95%+ by 2022.

Push-to-Card (Real-Time Debit Funding)

Visa Direct and Mastercard Send are card network programs that allow originators to push funds to a debit card number in near real-time — typically within minutes. Unlike ACH, no bank routing number or account number is needed; just the 16-digit debit card number. This has become the backbone of the "instant payout" economy.

How It Works

  • Originator submits a push-to-card request with recipient's debit card number, amount, and originator's account details
  • Network (Visa/MC) routes the credit to the issuing bank of the debit card
  • Issuing bank credits the cardholder's DDA (checking account) — available within 30 minutes typically
  • Settlement occurs overnight via standard card settlement (the originator's account is debited the following business day)
  • Limits: Typically $10,000–$15,000 per transaction, $50,000 per day (vary by issuer and originator agreement)
  • Not all debit cards accept push: Issuer must enable "Original Credit Transaction" (OCT) capability on the card BIN

Credit Cards — Overview & History

Credit cards are revolving lines of credit — consumers spend up to a set credit limit and repay the balance in full (avoiding interest) or carry a balance (incurring APR). The credit card industry is one of the most profitable businesses in financial services, generating revenue through interest, interchange, and fees.

3.2

Credit Card Economics

Revenue Streams for Issuers

💸
Interest Income (Finance Charges)
The largest revenue source. APR applied to revolving balances. Average APR ~22% (2024). ~55% of cardholders carry a balance each month ("revolvers"). ~45% pay in full ("transactors") — the industry calls them "deadbeats" because they generate interchange but no interest.
💱
Interchange Revenue
Issuer earns 1.5–2.5%+ on each credit transaction. Critical for funding rewards programs. Premium cards (CSR, Amex Platinum) earn ~2.3–3.5% interchange — partly subsidizing rich rewards.
🧾
Annual Fees
Ranges from $0 (mass market) to $695 (Amex Centurion). Premium cards with annual fees generate predictable fee revenue independent of spending. Cardholders with high annual fees tend to be the most profitable — high spend, high interchange, premium rewards costs offset by fee and spend volume.
⚠️
Late & Penalty Fees
Late payment fees (CARD Act cap: ~$30 first offense, $41 subsequent). Penalty APR: Up to 29.99%, applied after missed payments. Cash advance fees: 3–5% of advance amount. Foreign transaction fees: 1–3% per international transaction.

The Credit Card P&L Framework

+ Interest income (APR × revolving balances)
+ Interchange revenue
+ Annual fees
+ Late / penalty / transaction fees
− Rewards cost (cash back, points, miles)
− Funding costs (cost of funds for credit extended)
− Charge-offs (bad debt write-offs)
− Operating expenses (servicing, fraud, acquisition)
− Fraud losses
= Net Income (Risk-Adjusted Return)

3.4

Rewards Economics

Rewards programs are the central competitive battleground of the credit card industry. They exist because issuers use rewards to drive card spending, which generates interchange revenue. The economics of rewards programs are complex and fascinating.

Why Rewards Exist (The Incentive Loop)

1

Issuer Offers Rewards

Chase offers 3× points on dining with the Sapphire Preferred. This incentivizes cardholders to use the Sapphire card — rather than cash or a competitor card — when eating out.

2

More Spending = More Interchange

Every swipe at a restaurant earns Chase ~2% interchange. Over a year, heavy dining spend generates significant interchange revenue.

3

Interchange Funds Rewards

Chase uses interchange revenue to fund the points. A Chase UR point costs Chase approximately 0.8–1.2 cents to issue. If redeemed at 1.5 cents via the travel portal, Chase funds the delta from its margins.

4

Breakage Subsidizes the Program

Breakage (points that expire or are never redeemed) subsidizes the rewards program. Typical breakage rates: 10–20% of issued points are never redeemed. Airlines and hotels use breakage as a significant profit lever in their loyalty programs.

💡

The Regressive Nature of Card Rewards

Rewards programs have a regressive economic effect: consumers who pay with cash or debit (typically lower income) effectively subsidize rewards for premium cardholders. Merchants cannot (in most US states, despite surcharging rules) charge different prices for card vs. cash — so the merchant's MDR cost is embedded in prices paid by everyone, while only card users capture the rewards. This dynamic has been extensively studied by economists and is a core argument in the ongoing interchange regulation debate.

3.5

Chargebacks & Dispute Resolution

A chargeback is the reversal of a credit card transaction, initiated by the cardholder through their issuing bank. It is the card industry's core consumer protection mechanism — but also one of the most significant operational challenges for merchants. Estimated annual chargeback losses to merchants exceed $40 billion globally.

The Chargeback Process

1

Cardholder Files Dispute

Cardholder contacts issuer (within 120 days of transaction date for most reason codes) to dispute a charge. Issuer opens a dispute case and provides provisional credit to the cardholder.

2

Issuer Files Chargeback

Issuer sends a chargeback to the card network with a reason code. The transaction amount is debited from the acquiring bank.

3

Acquirer Notifies Merchant

Acquirer debits the merchant's account and provides notice of the chargeback with reason code, evidence requirements, and response deadline (typically 30–45 days).

4

Merchant Re-presents (or Accepts)

Merchant can accept the chargeback (no response) or fight it by submitting compelling evidence: signed receipts, shipping proof, communication records, IP address data, fraud prevention system data. This is called "representment."

5

Arbitration (If Needed)

If the issuer rejects the re-presentment, the case can escalate to network arbitration. The losing party pays an arbitration fee (~$500). Most merchants accept losses rather than pay arbitration fees on small transactions.

Friendly Fraud: The Growing Epidemic

An estimated 30–50% of all chargebacks are friendly fraud — where the cardholder knowingly disputes a legitimate transaction. Common patterns: consumer receives goods but claims they didn't; consumer forgets the charge; consumer intentionally commits fraud to get free goods. Merchants combat this with: compelling transaction evidence, delivery confirmation, behavioral analytics, and representment automation platforms (Chargebacks911, Verifi, Ethoca).

Chargeback Monitoring Programs

Visa and Mastercard actively monitor merchant chargeback rates. Thresholds:

  • Visa Chargeback Monitoring Program (VCMP): >100 chargebacks/month AND >0.9% chargeback rate → Early Warning; >1% → Standard; >2% → Excessive
  • Mastercard Excessive Chargeback Merchant (ECM): >100 chargebacks/month AND >1.5% → ECM; >3% → HECM (High ECM)
  • Consequences: Financial fines ($25–$100/chargeback); mandatory fraud prevention measures; potential termination from card acceptance (placed on MATCH list)

3D Secure (3DS) — Liability Shift

3D Secure (Visa Secure / Mastercard Identity Check) is an authentication protocol for card-not-present transactions. When the issuer successfully authenticates the cardholder during checkout (via OTP, biometric, or passive risk-based authentication), the fraud liability shifts from the merchant to the issuer. This is the primary tool merchants use to reduce CNP chargeback exposure.

4.1

Types of Prepaid Cards

Prepaid cards are cards loaded with value before use — there is no credit underwriting, no linked bank account, and often no minimum balance requirement. They serve as a critical financial tool for the unbanked and underbanked population (~77 million US adults) while also powering sophisticated corporate payment programs.

🔓 Open-Loop (Network-Branded)

Carry Visa, Mastercard, or Amex branding. Accepted at any merchant that accepts that network — worldwide.

  • GPR (General Purpose Reloadable) cards
  • Government benefit cards (EBT, SSI, unemployment)
  • Payroll cards
  • Health FSA/HSA/HRA cards
  • Virtual prepaid cards

🔒 Closed-Loop (Single Merchant)

Only accepted at the issuing merchant or affiliated merchants. No network branding.

  • Retail gift cards (Starbucks, Amazon, Target)
  • Restaurant gift cards
  • Merchant loyalty currency
  • Transit cards (Oyster, ORCA, BART)

4.2

Regulatory Framework

⚠️

Prepaid Cards Historically Had Minimal Consumer Protection

Before 2019, prepaid cards occupied a regulatory gray zone. Reg E did not fully apply; error resolution was patchwork; fee disclosures were inconsistent and buried. This left millions of unbanked consumers — often the most financially vulnerable — with weaker protections than bank account holders.

CFPB Prepaid Rule (Effective April 2019)

The CFPB's Prepaid Accounts Rule extended meaningful Reg E protections to prepaid accounts for the first time. Key requirements:

  • Error resolution: Same Reg E unauthorized transaction protections as debit cards — consumer reports error, bank investigates within 10 business days, provisional credit if needed
  • Short-form fee disclosure: Standardized "Prepaid Account Fee Schedule" must be provided pre-purchase with key fees highlighted (monthly, per-purchase, ATM withdrawal, reload, inactivity)
  • Long-form fee disclosure: Complete list of all fees in prescribed format, available online and on request
  • 30-day waiting period: If a prepaid account offers a linked credit feature (overdraft), the credit cannot be activated for 30 days after account registration (anti-predatory lending measure)
  • Periodic statements or online access: 12 months of transaction history must be available

FDIC Pass-Through Insurance

GPR prepaid cards can carry FDIC insurance, but only if properly structured. The issuing bank must be FDIC-insured, the funds must be held in properly titled accounts, and accurate records of individual cardholder balances must be maintained. When these conditions are met, each cardholder is covered up to $250,000. Green Dot Bank is itself FDIC-insured as a bank, making its products straightforwardly covered.

AML / BSA Considerations

Prepaid cards have historically been a money laundering risk (purchase with cash, load value, spend anonymously). FinCEN regulations require:

  • Cards with load limits over $10,000/month or no expiration typically require CIP (Customer Identification Program) / KYC
  • International transfer-capable prepaid cards face enhanced scrutiny
  • The Prepaid Access Rule (2011) requires "prepaid access providers" to register with FinCEN as MSBs

The Small Bank / Durbin Arbitrage

The most important economic feature of GPR prepaid: because most GPR programs are issued through banks under $10 billion in assets (e.g., Bancorp Bank, Pathward/MetaBank, The Bancorp), they are exempt from the Durbin cap. This means GPR prepaid debit cards earn full market-rate interchange (~$0.50–$0.80 per transaction vs. the Durbin-capped ~$0.21–$0.24 for large bank debit). This is how neobanks like Chime, Current, and Dave fund their "free" business models.

Key Industry Participants

Program Managers

Handle distribution, marketing, customer service, and program administration. They work with an issuing bank as the licensed financial institution. Examples: NetSpend (owned by Global Payments), InComm, Blackhawk Network, Green Dot (also its own bank).

Bank Issuers (BIN Sponsors)

Provide the banking license, BIN, network membership, and regulatory compliance. Revenue-sharing with program manager. Examples: The Bancorp Bank, Pathward (MetaBank), Green Dot Bank, Evolve Bank & Trust, Cross River Bank.

4.4

Virtual & Single-Use Cards

Virtual prepaid cards represent the most rapidly growing segment of the prepaid market — driven by the need for secure, trackable B2B payments and the explosion of corporate AP automation.

🔢
Virtual Card
A 16-digit card number, expiry, and CVV — with no physical plastic. Used for CNP (card-not-present / online) transactions. Can be issued instantly, configured with spend controls, and tracked at the transaction level.
1️⃣
Single-Use Virtual Card (SUVC)
Generated for exactly one transaction, then invalidated. Even if the number is stolen, it's worthless. Primary use: Corporate AP payments to suppliers, travel bookings, online purchases where security is paramount.
🔄
Account Updater
A Visa/Mastercard network service that automatically pushes updated card numbers to enrolled merchants when a card is reissued (lost, expired, compromised). Reduces failed recurring payments. Critical for subscription businesses.
🔒
Network Tokenization
Card number replaced with a network token for each merchant relationship. Token is merchant-specific: if stolen from one merchant, useless at others. Powers Apple Pay, Google Pay, and digital wallet security. Dramatically reduces CNP fraud.
🏢
5.1

Commercial Card Program Types

Commercial cards are cards issued for business purposes — controlling employee spending, paying suppliers, managing fleet costs, and automating accounts payable. The US commercial card market exceeded $1.2 trillion in spend in 2023 and continues growing rapidly as organizations replace checks and paper processes with card-based automation.

✈️
Corporate / T&E Card
Travel & entertainment spending by employees. American Express dominates. Often individual liability — employee responsible for payment; company reimburses. Integrates with expense management tools.
🛒
Purchasing Card (P-Card)
High-volume procurement. Company liability. MCC and spend controls. Level 3 data capability. Replaces purchase orders and checks for routine buying. Primary driver of commercial card spend growth.
⛽
Fleet Card
Fuel and vehicle maintenance for company fleets. MCC-restricted to gas stations and auto services. Captures odometer data. Closed-loop networks (WEX, Fleetcor) or open-loop with fleet controls.
👻
Ghost / Lodge Card
No physical card; account number pre-shared with specific recurring vendors (hotels, airlines). Used for managed travel programs where a central account pays specific suppliers automatically.
💻
Virtual Card (AP Automation)
Single-use or controlled virtual card numbers issued by AP automation platforms to pay suppliers. Replacing paper checks in B2B payables. Issuer earns interchange; some shared with buyer as rebate.
🔗
One Card
Combines T&E and purchasing capabilities into a single card. Simplifies program administration and card management for midsize companies.

Charge Card vs. Revolving

Many corporate cards — particularly Amex — are charge cards, not revolving credit. The full balance is due each month; there is no preset spending limit (NPSL). This means the card's limit scales with the company's spending pattern, and there are no interest charges — but also no option to carry a balance. Corporate charge cards are structured differently from consumer credit cards and are typically outside the scope of the CARD Act.

Level 2 and Level 3 Data

Commercial cards can capture and transmit enhanced transaction data through the payment network — enabling lower interchange rates and better spend visibility:

  • Level 1 (Standard): Basic data — merchant name, amount, date. Consumer interchange rate applies.
  • Level 2: Adds sales tax amount, customer reference code, merchant postal code. ~0.5% interchange reduction for B2B transactions.
  • Level 3: Full line-item detail — item description, quantity, unit cost, product code, freight amount. ~1%+ interchange reduction. Requires both merchant and acquirer to support L3 data. Critical for P-card programs.

Expense Management Integration

Modern corporate card programs integrate tightly with expense management platforms. Market leaders: Concur (SAP), Expensify, TripActions/Navan. Challenger platforms with card-first approaches: Brex, Ramp, Divvy (BILL). These platforms provide real-time transaction feeds, automated receipt matching, policy enforcement, and manager approval workflows — dramatically reducing the cost and friction of expense management.

5.3

Purchasing Cards (P-Cards)

Purchasing cards are the engine of commercial card spend growth. They replace traditional procurement processes (purchase orders, invoice approval, check payment) for routine, low-to-medium-dollar purchases — streamlining procurement and generating rebate income for buyers.

Controls Available on P-Cards

🏪
MCC Restrictions
Restrict card to specific Merchant Category Codes. Example: IT department P-card blocked at restaurants and auto dealers; only allowed at office supply stores, software vendors, electronics retailers.
💲
Transaction Limits
Per-transaction ceiling. Anything above requires purchase order. Common threshold: $2,500–$10,000. Balances controls against procurement efficiency.
📅
Daily / Monthly Limits
Rolling spend caps to prevent abuse. Automatically resets on billing cycle. Can be adjusted by program administrator per employee or department.
👤
Employee-Specific Controls
Different limits and restrictions for different employees or departments. Admin can increase limits temporarily for specific projects.

The Rebate Economics

One of the most compelling — and often misunderstood — aspects of commercial cards is the rebate program. Banks share interchange revenue with corporate buyers as an incentive to use cards instead of checks:

  • Commercial interchange: 2.5–3.5% on most commercial card transactions (much higher than consumer debit)
  • Buyer rebate: Bank returns 0.5–1.5% of spend to the corporate buyer annually
  • Net to issuer: After rebate and processing costs, issuer retains the remainder — still profitable
  • Buyer ROI: For a $10 million annual P-card program at 1% rebate, the buyer earns $100,000 — often exceeding the cost of the program administration
  • Working capital benefit: Buyers also get 30–55 days of float before payment is due; suppliers receive payment faster than traditional AP terms
⛽
5.4

Fleet Cards

Fleet cards manage fuel and vehicle maintenance spending for organizations with vehicle fleets — from small delivery companies to massive trucking fleets of thousands of vehicles.

🔒 Closed-Loop Fleet Networks

Accepted only at partner fuel stations. Lower cost for merchants (no standard card interchange). Feature-rich for fleet management.

  • WEX Inc. (formerly Wright Express) — largest closed-loop fleet network
  • Fleetcor — Fuelman, FleetCards USA brands
  • Comdata — trucking industry focus; also ATM access
  • EFS (Elavon Fleet Solutions)

🌐 Open-Loop Fleet (Visa/MC-Branded)

Accepted anywhere the network is accepted, with fleet management controls layered on. More flexibility but typically higher cost and fewer fleet-specific features.

  • Useful for diverse, geographically dispersed fleets
  • WEX also offers open-loop Visa products
  • Standard interchange applies; fleet controls via card parameters

Fleet-Specific Data Capture

  • Odometer reading: Driver enters odometer at pump; captured and transmitted with transaction. Enables MPG tracking and maintenance scheduling.
  • Driver ID / vehicle ID: PIN-based identification ties each transaction to a specific driver and vehicle
  • Fuel grade restriction: Card can be restricted to regular unleaded only (prevents premium purchases)
  • IFTA reporting: International Fuel Tax Agreement compliance for interstate truckers — fuel purchase data by state automatically aggregated
  • Telematics integration: Increasingly, fleet card data integrates with GPS telematics (Samsara, Verizon Connect) for comprehensive fleet cost analytics

5.5

AP Automation — The B2B Card Opportunity

Accounts payable automation via virtual cards represents one of the largest greenfield opportunities in commercial payments. Despite decades of electronic payments adoption, an estimated ~40–50% of US B2B payments are still made by paper check. Each check costs $3–20 to process; virtual card costs under $0.50. The economic case for conversion is clear — but supplier adoption has been the friction point.

How Virtual Card AP Works

1

Invoice Received

Buyer's AP system receives invoice from supplier (via EDI, AP automation platform, or manual upload).

2

Virtual Card Generated

AP platform (AvidXchange, Billtrust, MineralTree, SAP Ariba) generates a single-use virtual card number tied to the exact invoice amount and the specific supplier. Controls: can only be charged by this merchant, for this exact amount, within a narrow time window.

3

Card Number Delivered to Supplier

The virtual card number is sent to the supplier via secure email, supplier portal, or automated data feed. Supplier charges the card through their standard card terminal or virtual terminal.

4

Supplier Receives Funds T+1

Standard card settlement: supplier's bank receives net proceeds (less their MDR) within 1-2 business days. Faster than net-30 or net-60 check terms.

5

Buyer Pays Card Statement

Buyer pays the card issuer on net-30 terms. During the float period, buyer retains working capital. Annual rebate is credited at year-end.

The Supplier Acceptance Problem

The primary obstacle to virtual card AP adoption is supplier resistance to paying the MDR. A supplier on thin margins cannot afford to lose 2.5–3.5% of each invoice. Solutions being deployed: (1) Dynamic discounting — buyer offers early payment discount in exchange for card acceptance; (2) Blended programs — high-MDR suppliers pay via ACH instead, maintaining the relationship; (3) Supplier education — calculating that faster cash flow has a working capital value that offsets MDR cost.
